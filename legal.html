AI Tools for Legal Counsel

Contract drafting, legal research, discovery review, and client communication. I tested each tool on tasks lawyers actually do, not demos. Here is what is worth your time and what carries risk you cannot ignore.

By Richard Migliorisi · Fact-checked by Ryan Cooper · March 2026

What AI Actually Does for Lawyers

Legal work is document-intensive at every stage: drafting, reviewing, researching, communicating, and advising. AI does not replace attorney judgment, but it compresses each stage significantly. These are the specific tasks where working attorneys are seeing real time savings.

DRAFTING

Contract and NDA First Drafts

Describe the deal structure, parties, and key terms and ChatGPT or Claude produces a solid first draft in seconds. Attorneys consistently report saving 60 to 90 minutes per agreement. Always treat the output as a first draft requiring attorney review, not a final work product.

Try: ChatGPT or Claude
REVIEW

Contract Review and Issue-Spotting

Claude's 200,000-token context window can ingest entire agreements in a single prompt. Ask it to identify unusual indemnification language, missing standard provisions, or clauses that conflict with your standard positions. It dramatically accelerates the first pass before you do substantive review.

Try: Claude (best for full-length documents)
DISCOVERY

Deposition and Document Summarization

Paste a deposition transcript or lengthy filing into Claude or ChatGPT and ask for a structured summary: key testimony, inconsistencies, admissions, and items requiring follow-up. Useful for preparing for cross-examination or briefing supervising attorneys on large document review batches.

Try: Claude for long transcripts, ChatGPT for shorter ones
COMMUNICATION

Client Letters and Demand Drafts

AI drafts client-facing letters, status updates, and demand letters in the appropriate register. Provide the legal facts and desired outcome; the draft handles structure and language. Run the output through Grammarly for tone polish before sending. You ensure legal accuracy and professional responsibility.

Try: ChatGPT or Claude, then Grammarly to polish

BEFORE YOU START

Do not paste client-identifiable information into consumer AI tools. ChatGPT Free, Claude.ai, and Perplexity AI free tiers do not offer the contractual data protections your firm needs. Review your firm's AI policy before using any tool on client matters. When inputting documents, anonymize or use enterprise plans with appropriate data processing agreements.

ChatGPT

Recommended

Made by OpenAI

Strong general-purpose drafting assistant. Produces solid first drafts of contracts, engagement letters, and client emails. Hallucinates case citations reliably, so never trust its legal research output.

  • First-draft contracts and NDAs
  • Client intake and communication emails
  • Document summarization (paste text in)
  • Issue-spotting from fact patterns

Claude

Recommended

Made by Anthropic

The 200K-token context window is the real differentiator: you can feed an entire contract, deposition transcript, or case file in one pass. Better at following complex multi-part instructions than ChatGPT.

  • Full-contract review in a single prompt
  • Deposition and discovery summarization
  • Multi-clause comparison and redlining
  • Research synthesis (not citation generation)

Perplexity AI

Recommended

Made by Perplexity

The only general AI tool that provides verifiable citations. Useful for identifying relevant areas of law and locating secondary sources. Not a Westlaw or LexisNexis replacement, but a fast first-pass research tool.

  • Identifying relevant statutes and regulations
  • Finding secondary sources and law review articles
  • Jurisdiction-level research starting points
  • Tracking recent case developments

Recommended

Made by Google

Competitive context window and strong document handling. Most useful if your firm is on Google Workspace. Less specialized for legal tasks than Claude, but capable for drafting and summarization.

Common Questions

What professionals ask before adopting AI tools.

Can AI tools replace legal research databases like Westlaw or LexisNexis?

No. General AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude fabricate case citations. Perplexity AI provides real citations but cannot replace the depth, reliability, and jurisdiction-specific accuracy of Westlaw or LexisNexis. Use AI for research starting points and document drafting, not as your primary legal research tool.

Is it safe to paste client documents into AI tools?

Not without precautions. Review your firm's data policies before pasting client information into any AI. Enterprise versions of ChatGPT, Claude, and Copilot offer contractual data protections, but the free consumer versions do not. Do not paste confidential client data unless you are using an approved secure plan.

Which AI tool is best for contract review?

Claude leads for contract review due to its 200K-token context window, which lets you paste an entire agreement in a single prompt. ChatGPT is also strong but handles shorter documents more reliably. Both require human review of all output before relying on it professionally.

What legal tasks should I never use AI for?

Do not use AI to verify case citations, interpret jurisdiction-specific procedural rules, or make final judgment calls on compliance or risk. AI tools are drafting assistants and research accelerators. Professional judgment, verification, and responsibility remain with the attorney.

Can AI summarize discovery documents and depositions?

Yes, and this is one of the highest-ROI uses for litigation attorneys. Paste a transcript or document batch into Claude and ask for a structured summary organized by topic, witness, or timeline. The output accelerates review and flags areas needing deeper attention. Always verify key admissions against the source text before relying on the summary in filings or court.

How should a lawyer get started with AI tools?

Start with tasks that carry no confidentiality risk: drafting cover letters, writing plain-English summaries of public statutes, or generating first-draft engagement letters from a template. Build familiarity and judgment before moving to client-matter work. Review your firm's technology and data policy before using any AI tool on active matters.

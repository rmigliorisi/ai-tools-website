Grammarly for Lawyers: An Honest Assessment (2026)

What Grammarly actually does for legal professionals, where it adds genuine value, and where ChatGPT or Claude is the better choice. Plus prompts and Grammarly goal settings that work in legal writing.

Specialized Tool March 19, 2026 7 min read

By Richard Migliorisi · Fact-checked by Ryan Cooper · March 19, 2026

Bottom Line

Grammarly is a specialized writing quality tool, not a general AI assistant. For lawyers, it excels at polishing briefs, client letters, and correspondence that you have already drafted. It does not replace ChatGPT or Claude for generating content. Treat it as the last step in your workflow, not the first.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Grammarly works where you already write (Word, Outlook, Gmail, Chrome). Unlike ChatGPT, there is no copy-paste friction. It is always on, which means it catches errors in documents you would not otherwise take the time to review.

BEST USE CASES

  • Polishing briefs, motions, and pleadings
  • Toning client letters from formal to accessible
  • Firm-wide style and terminology consistency
  • Real-time editing as you type in Word and Gmail

AVOID IF

  • You need to generate content from scratch
  • You need legal research or case analysis
  • You are looking for a ChatGPT or Claude alternative
  • You need Bluebook or citation format checking

MINI WORKFLOW

Draft with ChatGPT or Claude. Paste into Word with Grammarly active. Set audience to Expert, formality to Formal. Review suggestions, accepting those that improve clarity and rejecting those that conflict with deliberate legal style choices. Run final client letters through the tone detector before sending.

What Grammarly Does Well for Legal Professionals

Grammarly is not an AI assistant in the ChatGPT sense. It does not generate content, research cases, or answer legal questions. What it does is review and improve text you have already produced. In a legal workflow, that is a distinct and valuable function.

Brief and Motion Polishing

Briefs written under deadline pressure accumulate passive voice, unclear pronoun references, and inconsistent terminology. Grammarly Premium catches these systematically before the document goes to opposing counsel or the court. Setting the audience to Expert and the register to Formal minimizes suggestions that would conflict with professional legal style.

Tone Calibration for Client Communication

The same attorney writes a court-filing brief and a client status update letter. The register shift between those documents is significant: court filings are dense and formal, client letters need to be accessible. Grammarly's tone detector flags when client-facing documents still read like pleadings, which is one of the most common communication failures in legal practice.

No-Friction Editing Everywhere You Write

Grammarly runs as a browser extension and integrates natively with Word, Outlook, and Gmail. Unlike ChatGPT or Claude, which require you to open a separate interface and copy text back and forth, Grammarly catches errors in real time as you type. For high-volume correspondence work, this passive quality improvement adds up significantly.

Firm-Wide Style Consistency (Business Plan)

Grammarly Business allows firms to create custom style guides: preferred terminology, phrases to avoid, standard disclaimer language, and consistent partner voice across all outgoing documents. This is particularly valuable for firms where multiple attorneys correspond with the same client and inconsistency in language creates confusion or weakens the firm's professional image.

Grammarly Settings and Workflows to Try

Grammarly does not take traditional prompts, but configuring its goals correctly makes suggestions far more relevant for legal writing. Use these as starting settings:

Goals for court filings and briefs

Audience: Expert
Formality: Formal
Domain: Academic
Tone: Neutral

After setting goals, review for: passive voice overuse, unclear pronoun references, any sentence that could be read two ways in a legal context. Override suggestions that conflict with deliberate stylistic choices or citation conventions.

Goals for client-facing correspondence

Audience: General
Formality: Neutral
Domain: General
Tone: Confident, Friendly

Flag sentences longer than 35 words. Look for: legal Latin not translated for clients, passive voice that obscures who is responsible for next steps, and jargon that requires legal training to understand.

ChatGPT prompt to pair with Grammarly review

Rewrite this client update letter at a Grade 8 reading level. Use active voice throughout. Replace all Latin phrases with plain English equivalents. Preserve the legal accuracy of every substantive statement. Do not add or remove any factual claims. Output only the rewritten letter.

[PASTE LETTER]

Where Grammarly Falls Short for Lawyers

✗

No content generation

Grammarly cannot draft contracts, write briefs, or generate first-pass documents. For that, use ChatGPT or Claude. Grammarly improves what you have already written; it does not create anything from scratch.

✗

Does not understand Bluebook or legal citation format

Grammarly may flag or incorrectly suggest changes to citation formats. It does not understand legal citation conventions, so override any suggestions that touch citations, case names, or jurisdiction-specific abbreviations.

✗

Suggestions sometimes conflict with legal style choices

Grammarly does not know that "shall" is a deliberate contractual choice or that a long sentence is a strategic rhetorical move in a brief. The suggestion to simplify or restructure can be wrong. Attorneys need judgment about which suggestions to accept.

✗

Free tier is too limited for professional use

The free version provides basic spelling and grammar checks only. Tone detection, advanced suggestions, and style goals require Premium or Business. For professional legal use, the free tier is insufficient.

Common Questions

Answers start with Yes, No, or It depends.

Is Grammarly useful for lawyers?

Yes, for specific tasks. Grammarly is a writing quality and editing tool, not a content generator. It excels at polishing briefs, client letters, and correspondence that you have already drafted. It does not replace ChatGPT or Claude for generating content and is best used as the final step in your writing workflow.

Can Grammarly draft legal documents?

No. Grammarly is an editor and polisher, not a content generator. It reviews and improves text you have already written or generated with another AI tool. For drafting contracts, letters, and briefs from scratch, use ChatGPT or Claude, then run the output through Grammarly before sending.

Is Grammarly safe for client information?

It depends on the plan. Consumer plans process text on Grammarly servers without enterprise data agreements. Grammarly Business offers data processing agreements suitable for professional use. Do not paste confidential client information into the free consumer version. Review your firm's AI policy and Grammarly's current data terms before use on client matters.

Does Grammarly understand legal writing conventions?

Partially. Setting Audience to Expert and Formality to Formal reduces suggestions that conflict with legal style. However, Grammarly does not understand Bluebook citation format, legal Latin conventions, or jurisdiction-specific requirements. Override any suggestions that touch citations, contractual terms like "shall," or deliberate rhetorical choices in briefs.

What is Grammarly Business and is it worth it for law firms?

It depends on firm size. Grammarly Business adds team-level style guides, custom terminology enforcement, and enterprise data agreements. For firms wanting consistent voice across multiple attorneys writing to the same clients, it provides measurable quality improvement. Worth evaluating for firms with 3 or more regular users.

How does Grammarly compare to ChatGPT or Claude for legal work?

They serve different functions and work best together. ChatGPT and Claude generate content, draft documents, and analyze text. Grammarly reviews and improves existing text in real time. The best workflow combines them: use ChatGPT or Claude to draft first, then Grammarly to polish the output before it goes to clients, opposing counsel, or the court.

WHAT MOST REVIEWS MISS

1.

The no-friction advantage is the real value proposition. Most AI reviews focus on what Grammarly cannot do compared to ChatGPT. The more relevant point is that Grammarly runs everywhere you already write with zero workflow disruption. For attorneys who will not change their writing environment to use AI, Grammarly is the AI tool they will actually adopt.

2.

The best Grammarly workflow pairs it with ChatGPT or Claude, not instead of them. Reviews usually position Grammarly as an alternative to AI writing tools. The correct mental model is: ChatGPT or Claude generate the draft, Grammarly polishes it. The combination is stronger than either alone, especially for client-facing correspondence where tone and precision both matter.

3.

Grammarly Business's style guide feature is underused in legal contexts. Most law firms do not configure it. Done properly, a firm style guide ensures that every attorney uses the firm's preferred terminology, standard disclaimer language, and consistent voice across all client communications. This creates measurable consistency improvement that is otherwise impossible to enforce at scale.
